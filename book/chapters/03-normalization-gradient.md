# Chapter 3 — The Normalization Gradient

If you walk into a room that smells strongly of paint, the smell is
overwhelming for the first minute and almost gone within ten. The
paint molecules are still there. The concentration in the air has
not changed. What has changed is your olfactory system, which has
recalibrated its baseline to whatever it is currently encountering
and stopped firing for differences below a certain threshold. The
smell is, in the only sense that matters to you, no longer there.

This is not a flaw in your nose. It is the central feature of how
the nervous system handles a continuously changing environment.
Sustained sensory input gets normalized, set as the new baseline,
and tuned out. Awareness is reserved for *change* — and not all
change, but change steep enough to exceed what the system is
currently treating as the local fluctuation range. The Arctic
explorer's body adapts to cold; the equatorial farmer's body
adapts to heat; the urban resident's hearing adapts to traffic;
the office worker's eyes adapt to fluorescent light. None of these
adaptations are voluntary. None of them are conscious. All of them
are continuously rewriting what *normal* means, against whatever
input is most recently present.

A fixed reference for *normal* would have killed our lineage. The
same body had to inhabit the savanna and the city, the cave and
the office, the campfire and the LED screen. A drifting reference,
retuned to whatever is most recently present, kept the lineage
alive. The cost of that adaptation is not metaphorical. **Things
that move slowly enough do not register as change. They register
as the world.**

This chapter is about that mechanism, and about what it means for
inherited frames in a moment when many of the most consequential
changes in the environment are deliberately calibrated to move at
exactly the rate that does not trigger our sensors.

## The species-level argument

The cognitive and biological literature on this has a long history
across several adjacent disciplines: psychophysics on sensory
adaptation, ecology on shifting baselines, behavioral economics on
hedonic adaptation, environmental history on the slow drift of
what counts as a normal landscape. The clearest single empirical
articulation in any of these literatures is in fisheries science,
in a three-page 1995 commentary by the marine biologist Daniel
Pauly titled *Anecdotes and the shifting baseline syndrome of
fisheries*.[^ch3-1]

Pauly's observation, framed for the discipline he had spent his
career inside, was that successive cohorts of fisheries
researchers were treating the species composition and abundance
they observed at the start of *their own* careers as the natural
baseline against which to measure decline. When they assessed
collapse, they assessed it against that baseline. The previous
generation's baseline — usually richer, sometimes by an order of
magnitude — was forgotten not through ignorance but through the
structural invisibility of slow change.

The diagnostic move is what makes the paper a tool that travels.
Pauly observed that the same fishery could simultaneously look
*destroyed* to an 1890s captain working from his lifetime of
memory and *fluctuating around historical norms* to a 1990s
fisheries manager working from data that began in 1970. Both
assessments were internally honest. The discrepancy was not in
observation but in *baseline*. The collapse was not invisible
because nobody saw it. It was invisible because the seeing
itself was structurally re-anchored every generation.

The recommendation Pauly made for fisheries is one this book will
return to: invest in retrieving the older baselines. Captains'
logs, port records, market records, archaeological evidence,
oral histories from retired fishers. Make those data part of the
working management framework, so that the discipline cannot
forget what *normal* used to look like even when no living
practitioner remembers.

The shifting-baseline mechanism, identified in fisheries, has
since been recognized in coastal-ecosystem science, in forest
ecology, in soils and freshwater systems, in urban green-space
planning, in conservation biology generally. It is now a
documented empirical pattern with a name and a citation history.
The argument of this chapter is that the same mechanism
generalizes well past ecology. It applies to inherited workdays,
classroom sizes, the steady extension of monitoring at work, the
steady contraction of what counts as professional judgment, and
the silently expanding scope of what algorithmic systems are
presumed to be doing in our institutions on our behalf.

## The shape of the gradient

Once you have the mechanism in hand, the world starts looking
sorted by gradient. Some changes are fast: a financial crash, a
hurricane, a coup, the announcement of a war. These trigger
many sensors at once. They are felt across body, society,
ecosystem, market, institutional rule-set, all reading
*out of adjacent normality* simultaneously. The dissonance is
what *feels like a paradigm shift*. The shock interval that
follows — usually weeks to a few months — is one of the rare
windows in which large structural rearrangements become
politically possible, because the normalization mechanism has
not yet caught up to the new state.

Other changes are slow. They move on a gradient shallow enough
that no single moment registers as a departure from the previous
moment. The sensors do not fire. The new state gets absorbed,
unchallenged, into the inherited frame. The result, on a
generational timescale, is a transformation as large as any
shock-induced rearrangement — but one that nobody specifically
deliberated about, because no specific moment ever felt like
the moment to deliberate.

The political question — *who steers the gradient* — is not
symmetric between the two cases. The same actor who would lose
a fast public argument can usually win a slow private one,
because nobody is looking at the slope. The eight-hour workday,
the thirty-students-per-classroom standard, and the
nine-to-five workweek were *won* by labor organizing in the
fast-gradient register. Their persistence after the conditions
that produced them disappeared is the slow-gradient story. So
is the steady extension, over the past two decades, of
algorithmic decision-making into hiring, lending, sentencing,
news distribution, and dating — none of which produced a public
debate of the kind generative AI did in 2022, despite all of
them being more consequential to more people on a longer
timescale.

The shock-and-normalize cycle is not just a description of how
change happens. It is, increasingly, an *instrument*. A way of
allocating which changes get democratic friction and which do
not.

## The political instrumentalization of the cycle

The journalist Naomi Klein gave the political-instrumentalization
argument its most influential statement in *The Shock Doctrine*
(2007). Her thesis, sharpened over the course of a long
historical analysis from Pinochet's Chile to post-Katrina New
Orleans, was that crisis intervals are increasingly being
*used* by actors who have learned to recognize them as
intervention windows. Klein traces the doctrine to the work of
Milton Friedman and the Chicago School, who argued explicitly
that *only crisis produces real change* and that the strategic
implication was to be ready with a pre-prepared policy package
when the crisis arrived. Friedman's phrase, which Klein returns
to throughout the book, was: *only a crisis — actual or
perceived — produces real change. When that crisis occurs, the
actions that are taken depend on the ideas that are lying
around.*[^ch3-2]

What Klein adds to the shock-and-normalize cycle is the
recognition that the rhythm has, for some actors, become a
known asset. The shock interval is planned for, sometimes
engineered, and the policy moves made during it are designed
in advance. The normalization that follows is then locked in
by structural changes that would not have survived the
deliberation the shock prevented.

The connection to the AI moment is direct and uncomfortable.
The release of ChatGPT in late 2022 functioned, in Klein's
frame, as a shock interval. A great deal of policy, capital
allocation, labor reorganization, and institutional adoption
happened on timescales that precluded ordinary deliberation.
Some of that was inevitable — the technology was real, the
demand was real, the response had to happen on some timescale.
Some of it was the reflexive use of the shock interval by
actors who had been ready for it for years. Distinguishing
the two is one of the things this book will try to help you
do, and one of the things the broader conversation has been
unable to do well.

## The other side of the cycle: paradigm shifts

The mirror image of slow-gradient invisibility is the rare
moment when a change exceeds the normalization range fast
enough that the inherited frame itself comes apart. This is
the move Thomas Kuhn named, in 1962, with the phrase that
has become, against his wishes, one of the most over-used
terms in contemporary culture: the *paradigm shift*.[^ch3-3]

Kuhn's argument was specific and worth recovering from its
popular reception. Science, in his account, does not progress
through the steady accumulation of facts. It alternates
between long stretches of *normal science* — in which a
community works inside a shared paradigm of exemplars,
instruments, problem-types, and tacit assumptions — and rare
moments in which the accumulated anomalies become unbearable
and a new paradigm replaces the old. The new paradigm is not
simply *better*. It is incommensurable: it organizes the
field around different problems, different criteria of
success, different exemplars of good work. Normal-science
practitioners working inside the older paradigm often cannot
fully see the new one even when they try, because the
perceptual furniture they were trained with was built from
the older paradigm's assumptions.

What this book borrows from Kuhn is the recognition that the
shock end of the gradient is structurally rare, expensive,
and resisted, even by communities that profess to value it.
Most communities do not undergo paradigm shifts; they
undergo replacement of practitioners by the next generation,
who were trained inside the new paradigm and never had to
unlearn the old. Kuhn was frank about this: paradigm shifts
happen through generational replacement as often as through
persuasion. This has uncomfortable implications for any
project of the kind this book is engaged in, and we will
return to those implications in the closing chapters.

For now, what matters is the structure: shock-and-normalize
on one side, slow-gradient invisibility on the other. The
sensor-firing range in between is the only zone in which
ordinary deliberation is usually possible, and most
contemporary change is being deliberately calibrated to stay
out of it.

## Why this is the book's central argument about change

The first two chapters of the book established that capacity
is environmental and that inherited frames calcify into
infrastructure. This chapter has named the cognitive and
biological mechanism by which the calcification happens
without anyone noticing. The combination is what the
framework will use, through the rest of the book, to read
the AI moment.

When this book makes the claim, in later chapters, that
generative AI was not the most consequential AI deployment
of the past fifteen years — that the recommender systems
silently reorganizing hiring, lending, news, and dating were
much more consequential — it is making a normalization-gradient
claim. The recommender systems moved on a gradient shallow
enough to never trigger broad debate. Generative AI moved on
a gradient steep enough to trigger every sensor at once. The
public attention is calibrated to the gradient, not to the
consequence. The work the book wants to help you do is to
notice this asymmetry and to compensate for it deliberately.

When the book makes the claim, in still later chapters, that
the AI moment is both genuinely a paradigm shift and *also*
an opportunistic shock interval being used by specific actors
to lock in arrangements they had been preparing for years, it
is making a Kuhn-meets-Klein claim. Both readings are needed.
Distinguishing them in any specific case is hard, and we will
not always be able to do it cleanly.

When the book asks, in the closing chapters, what it would
take to deliberately preserve the older baselines from which
contemporary AI deployment looks like a sharp departure rather
than a natural drift, it is asking the Pauly question
generalized. The work the framework wants to do is, in part,
to be the institutional analog of what fisheries science had
to become after Pauly: a discipline that deliberately
preserves the memory of what *normal* used to look like, even
when no living practitioner remembers.

## A small note on what this chapter is not saying

The normalization gradient is not a moral failing of the
people inside it. It is a feature of the nervous system
operating as designed, in environments it was not designed
for. Most readers of this book are themselves inside many
calcified frames they cannot see. So is the author. The
question is not whether to escape the mechanism — there is
no escape — but whether to build institutional practices
that compensate for it deliberately, and whether to be
honest about the political asymmetry of who benefits when
we do not.

The next chapter takes the question one level up. Inherited
frames do not exist only inside individuals or inside small
institutions. They exist at the scale of nations, religions,
markets, professional fields — units of cooperation much
larger than direct social cognition can hold. How human
beings came to coordinate at those scales, and what the
current technological transition is doing to the coordination
infrastructure, is the subject of Chapter 4.

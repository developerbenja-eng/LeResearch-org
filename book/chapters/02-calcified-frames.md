# Chapter 2 — Inherited Frames Calcify as Infrastructure

Eight glasses of water a day. Two thousand calories. Three meals on
a fixed schedule. Eight hours of sleep, continuous. The nine-to-five
workday, Monday through Friday. Thirty students per classroom,
sorted by birth year. Each of these is a number most adults treat
as a feature of the universe, like gravity. Each of them is, in
fact, a specific decision made by specific people for specific
reasons, most of which had nothing to do with what the human body
or mind actually needed, and almost none of which can be reproduced
on demand by the people currently living inside the decisions.

The eight-glasses-of-water rule traces back to a 1945 Food and
Nutrition Board recommendation that read *roughly one milliliter
of water per calorie of food intake* and noted that *most of this
quantity is contained in prepared foods*. The recommendation was
about total water intake, not drinking water, and it said so in
the original text. The number got copied without the qualifier
into popular health writing, and by the 1980s it had become
universal common sense. The qualifier — *most of it comes from
food* — had been forgotten. The number was preserved.[^1]

Two thousand calories is a 1993 FDA rounding decision for the
Nutrition Labeling and Education Act, chosen for label-printing
convenience. Nobody believed at the time that two thousand was
the right number for any specific person; the agency was looking
for a single round number against which to express percentages of
a daily value. The percentage got printed on every food label in
the United States. Within a generation, *two thousand calories*
had become the number Americans believed they were *supposed to
eat* — confidently enough that diet plans, fitness apps, and
restaurant menus organized themselves around it.

The three-meals-on-a-fixed-schedule pattern is a nineteenth-century
factory-shift convention, layered on top of older religious
mealtime traditions. Pre-industrial European societies typically
ate two larger meals or grazed across the day. The factory bell
required workers to eat at the same time so they could return to
the line at the same time. The factory bell is gone in most
contemporary work; the meal pattern remains, naturalized as
*how humans eat*.

Eight hours of continuous sleep is an industrial-era consolidation
of what had previously been *segmented sleep*: a *first sleep*
beginning a few hours after sundown, a wakeful interval of one to
two hours in the middle of the night during which people read,
prayed, conversed, or had sex, and a *second sleep* until dawn.
The historical record on segmented sleep is clear and goes back
centuries.[^2] The eight-hour-continuous-block expectation is
younger than the lightbulb. Insomniacs who wake up at three in
the morning and cannot fall back asleep are often experiencing
the natural human sleep pattern that the industrial schedule has
trained them to mistake for a disorder.

The nine-to-five workday is Henry Ford's 1914 decision at Ford
Motor Company, layered onto the mid-twentieth-century American
office-work convention. Ford's reasoning was specific: shorter
shifts kept workers more productive on the line and let them have
enough free time to consume the products the company was selling.
The decision was an industrial-scale management innovation, not a
discovery about human productivity. Cognitive work does not map
uniformly to clock hours. Most contemporary knowledge workers
know this from their own experience and continue to organize
their lives around the convention anyway.

Thirty students per classroom, age-graded, with fifty-minute
periods, is the Prussian-then-American compulsory-education model
from the middle of the nineteenth century. Nothing about how
human learning works requires uniform age cohorts, a thirty-to-one
student-to-teacher ratio, or fifty-minute periods. The choices
were administrative: they let one teacher manage one room of
similar-aged children efficiently, on a schedule compatible with
factory shifts.

Six numbers. Six decisions. Each one approximately right for some
people some of the time. None of them grounded in a discovery
about the human body or mind. All of them functioning, today, as
*infrastructure* — as load-bearing assumptions whose origins
nobody remembers and whose continued operation no one is
seriously questioning.

This is what it looks like for an inherited frame to calcify.

## What calcification means

When a contingent decision lasts long enough, it stops being a
decision. The first generation that lives with it remembers
making it; the second generation experiences it as the way things
are done; the third generation experiences it as the way things
*must* be done; and by the fourth generation, asking why it was
done that way sounds eccentric. The decision is now infrastructure.
The infrastructure is now invisible.

The sociologists Peter Berger and Thomas Luckmann gave this
process its most precise description in *The Social Construction
of Reality* (1966). They named it as a three-step rhythm.
*Externalization* is the first step: somebody does something
specific for some specific reason. *Objectivation* is the second:
the action is repeated, becomes a typification (*this is what
people like us do in this situation*), and then an institution
(*this is what is done*). *Internalization* is the third: new
members of the institution — children, recruits, new employees —
encounter it as a fully formed external reality, learn to navigate
it, and eventually identify with the roles it offers them.[^3]

The pattern is happening all the time, in every institution, at
every scale. The eight-glasses-of-water rule went through the
sequence in about fifty years. The thirty-students-per-classroom
convention went through it in about a hundred and fifty. The
nine-to-five workday is somewhere in the middle. The pattern
they all share is the structural forgetting: by the time the
participants in the institution are themselves doing the
typifying, the founders' specific reasons have dropped out of
memory. What remains is the role and the way one inhabits it.

Berger and Luckmann's deepest move is their account of what
they called *reification*: the moment when participants forget
that the institution is a human product at all and begin to
treat it as a feature of the universe. Reification is the
endpoint of the three-step cycle when no countervailing force
interrupts it. The eight-glasses-of-water rule is reified: most
people who follow it would, if asked, report that it is
*scientific* or *doctor-recommended* or *common knowledge*,
not that it is the residue of a misread 1945 advisory. The
thirty-students-per-classroom convention is reified: parents
who object to it are objecting *within* its framework
(*we should make classes smaller*), not to its framework
(*why should the unit of organization be a same-age cohort
in a single room with one teacher at all*).

The framework's earlier name for this — the one we will use
through the rest of the book — is that the contingent frame
*calcifies into infrastructure*. Bourdieu called the same
phenomenon *doxa*: the space of the taken-for-granted, the
assumptions so deeply shared they are not even available to be
debated.[^4] All three terminologies — Berger and Luckmann's
*reification*, Bourdieu's *doxa*, the framework's *calcified
frame* — are pointing at the same observation. A society
contains a great deal of background that nobody is currently
arguing about, that nobody can quite remember installing, and
that nevertheless does an enormous amount of work in shaping
what comes next.

## Why this matters now

The reason this matters now is that the *AI* category is in
the middle of going through this process in real time, on a
timescale much faster than any of the historical examples
above. The patterns of using a large language model to draft a
first version of every email, every report, every planning
document; the patterns of accepting AI-generated content
recommendations as the default informational diet; the
patterns of using algorithmic credit scoring, hiring filters,
or court-risk assessments as routine procedure rather than as
exceptional intervention — these patterns went from
externalization (somebody did it for a specific reason) to
typification (this is what people like us do) in about
eighteen months. They are now in objectivation: in many
institutions, *this is what is done* with AI is already a
settled question. The specific decisions that produced the
arrangement are still in living memory. They will not stay
there long.

If Berger and Luckmann's three-step rhythm normally takes
generations to run its course, the contemporary case is
running it in months. Whether the same vocabulary still
applies — whether *third-generation forgetting* still means
anything when the *generations* are quarterly product
release cycles — is one of the questions this book will
return to. But the basic shape of what is happening is
familiar. Decisions are calcifying into infrastructure, and
the infrastructure is becoming invisible at a pace the
historical record cannot quite prepare us for.

## The political character of calcification

It is tempting to read calcification as a neutral cognitive
process — *this is just what happens when humans repeat
behaviors* — and then to leave it at that. The framework
will not leave it at that, because calcification is not
neutral. It reliably favors the existing distribution of
power.

Bourdieu's sharpest political observation was that *doxa is
not symmetric*. The taken-for-granted reliably favors the
people who currently hold capital, status, and decision rights,
because those are the people whose preferences and assumptions
have had time to embed themselves as background. To make
something doxa is to take it off the table for argument. To
take it off the table for argument is to lock in whoever the
current arrangement benefits. The actors most invested in a
contingent frame becoming-doxa are usually the actors who
would lose any explicit argument about it.

This is why the eight-glasses-of-water rule does not
particularly trouble the bottled-water industry. It is why
the thirty-students-per-classroom convention does not
particularly trouble the testing-and-credentialing industry.
It is why the nine-to-five workday does not particularly
trouble the office-real-estate industry. The industries that
benefit from the frame becoming infrastructure are not
arguing for it explicitly. They do not have to. The frame is
already infrastructure. The work is done.

The implication for the AI moment is direct. The actors
currently most invested in a particular pattern of AI use
becoming infrastructure — the frontier labs, the platform
companies, the consultancies whose business models depend on
*AI integration* being the new universal — do not need to
make their case explicitly. They need only to ensure that
the patterns they prefer are repeated often enough, in
enough institutional contexts, to slip below the threshold
at which they could be argued about. The window for
arguing about them is now. The window will close as
calcification proceeds.

## What this chapter has not said

This chapter has not said that calcification is bad. It is
not. Some calcified frames are how a society holds together;
some are necessary background that frees attention for the
work that actually requires it. We do not want to be
deliberating, every morning, about whether to drive on the
right side of the road. We want that one to be infrastructure.

What this chapter has said is that *most of the operational
furniture of contemporary life* is calcified frames, that
their origins are forgotten, that their continued operation
is not seriously argued about, and that the cumulative effect
of this is an environment which is doing an enormous amount
of work shaping what we can think, want, and do — without
any of us being in a position to deliberately have made the
choices that built it.

If this is so, then the next question is *how* it stays
invisible. Why doesn't the calcification process fire any
sensors? Why don't we notice that the frame we are inside is
not the same as the frame our parents were inside, or our
children will be inside? The answer is in Chapter 3: the
cognitive and biological mechanism of normalization, which
makes change at low gradient invisible to us by design, and
which reserves our awareness for change steep enough to fire
many sensors at once.

# Chapter 8 — AI and Labor — The Wrong Question and the Right One

The question every reader of this book has heard, repeatedly, in
the past three years, is some version of: *will AI take my job?*
The question is asked by parents about their children. By
mid-career professionals about themselves. By policy researchers
in white papers. By management consultants in keynote slides. By
politicians at podiums. By labor unions in bargaining position
documents. By the people whose business model depends on the
answer being some version of *yes*.

The question is the wrong question. This chapter is about why,
and about what the right question is.

The wrongness is not that the question has no real referent.
Some jobs will be reorganized; some are already being reorganized;
the reorganization will be uneven and largely tracks pre-existing
inequalities. All of that is true. The wrongness is that the
question accepts as background a particular structure of *the
job* that is, on inspection, neither natural nor inevitable nor
permanent — and that accepting the structure as background
prevents us from asking the more important question, which is:
*which work was load-bearing in the first place, and which was
preserved for reasons unrelated to its output?*

That question, applied honestly to most contemporary white-collar
work, produces uncomfortable answers. The answers are the subject
of this chapter.

## The Graeber observations

The most extensive treatment of this in contemporary literature
is in the work of the late David Graeber, particularly in his
2013 essay *On the Phenomenon of Bullshit Jobs* and the longer
2018 book *Bullshit Jobs: A Theory*. Graeber, an anthropologist
who held a chair at the London School of Economics, was unusual
among academic theorists in his willingness to take workers'
own assessments of their own work seriously — not as data to be
theorized from outside but as analyses in their own right.[^ch8-1]

Graeber's central observation, drawn from a YouGov survey he
commissioned and from thousands of personal accounts received
after the 2013 essay went viral, was that approximately
thirty-seven percent of UK workers reported their own jobs as
making no meaningful contribution to the world. That is, not
*jobs that pay too little* or *jobs that are exploitative* (he
called those *shit jobs*, and treated them as a different
problem) — but jobs that the people performing them believed
to be *pointless*, that could disappear without consequence,
and that they could not admit were pointless without losing
the position.

The book proposed a typology of five categories:

*Flunkies* — jobs that exist primarily to make someone else
feel important. The receptionist at the company that receives
no visitors; the personal assistant whose actual function is
to signal the rank of the executive who has one.

*Goons* — jobs whose function is aggressive on behalf of an
employer in ways the worker finds ethically uncomfortable but
cannot avoid. Corporate lawyers, lobbyists, much of the
public-relations and advertising industries.

*Duct-tapers* — jobs whose function is to address problems
that should not exist in the first place. Fixing software
bugs that better engineering would have prevented; mediating
between departments whose poor coordination is a management
failure.

*Box-tickers* — jobs whose function is to make an organization
appear to be doing something it is not actually doing.
Compliance officers performing audits everyone knows are
theatrical; diversity consultants whose recommendations are
systematically ignored.

*Taskmasters* — jobs whose function is to supervise workers
who do not need supervision. Middle managers in flat-
organization tech companies; layers of project managers in
environments where the actual workers are competent and
self-organizing.

The book's deeper argument was that the persistence of these
categories is a problem orthodox economics has not adequately
confronted. Standard economic theory predicts that market
discipline would eliminate jobs that produce no value; the
empirical observation is that the bullshit-jobs sector has
*grown* over the decades during which market discipline has
supposedly been intensifying. Graeber's candidate explanations
included managerial feudalism (executives accumulating
subordinates as a form of status display), the financialization
of large companies (in which most employees are not exposed
to product-market competition), and — importantly for our
purposes — the political need to maintain high employment as
a discipline on labor generally, keeping people in jobs that
do not need doing rather than letting them have free time
that might be politically inconvenient.

## Why this matters for the AI question

The reason this matters for the AI question is direct. If a
significant fraction of contemporary white-collar work is
acknowledged-as-pointless even by the workers performing it,
then the question of *which jobs AI will take* has a
two-part answer that the standard discourse mostly avoids.

The first part: *AI is, in many cases, perfectly capable of
performing the bullshit jobs at scale*, because the bullshit
jobs were already the kind of work that consists of producing
plausible-sounding documents that nobody will read carefully,
producing meeting agendas that nobody needed, producing
compliance reports that exist to be filed rather than to
inform decisions. Generative AI is in fact rather good at
this. The first wave of AI-driven productivity gains in
white-collar work has, by available evidence, been
disproportionately gains in the production of bullshit. We
now produce more compliance reports per unit of human
attention than we used to, more meeting summaries, more
progress documents, more strategic-planning frameworks. None
of these actually serve the function they nominally serve.
The function they serve is the bullshit-job function: keeping
the structure of the job intact.

The second part: *the political function of the bullshit jobs
does not go away when AI can do them*. If the structural
purpose of the bullshit jobs is, at least in part, to keep
populations occupied with wage labor that does not need doing,
then automating the labor does not remove the political
problem. It reorganizes it. The new arrangement is one in
which the bullshit work is being done by AI under human
supervision — with the human doing the supervision being the
person who used to do the bullshit work, now in a slightly
different role with slightly less stability and slightly less
status. The job is not gone. The job is *transformed* in a
direction that preserves the political function while
eliminating the slow erosion of human dignity that came from
performing the original work.

Whether this is good news, bad news, or no news at all depends
on what one thought the original arrangement was for.

## The longer arc — Graeber on debt

Graeber's other major contribution to the framework, particularly
relevant when the *AI and labor* question is asked at the
broadest scale, is in his 2011 book *Debt: The First 5,000
Years*. The book's headline argument is that the standard
economic story about money — that it emerged from barter as a
more efficient medium of exchange — has no historical or
anthropological support. The actual sequence appears to have
been the reverse: extensive systems of credit and debt long
preceded the invention of currency, and the emergence of
physical money is more closely tied to states, armies, and the
management of war than to commercial convenience.[^ch8-2]

The relevance to the AI-and-labor question is the broader
argument the book makes, not its monetary specifics. Graeber
demonstrates, with cases from Mesopotamia to Madagascar to
medieval Europe to Tang-dynasty China, that the economic
categories we treat as background — debt, money, market, wage
labor itself — are recent and locally varied, that the present
arrangement is one of many that have existed, and that the
universality we project onto it is largely the work of the
last two or three centuries.

*Wage labor as identity* is, in this longer view, an exotic
recent arrangement, not a natural state of affairs to which
any deviation must be a deviation from human nature. The
contemporary structure in which a person's primary social
affiliation is their employer, in which their working hours
are their identity, in which their unemployment is a personal
failing rather than a structural condition, in which their
retirement is a discrete moment rather than a gradual
transition — all of this is a particular post-WWII
construction, layered on top of a particular industrial-
revolution construction, built on top of a particular
European-colonial construction. None of it is older than the
steam engine.

The implication for the AI-and-labor question is that the
structure being threatened by AI is itself a recent and local
arrangement. The question *will AI take your job?* assumes
that *your job*, in its current form, is the natural unit of
analysis. It is not. The natural unit of analysis is *the
work*, which has existed in many different organizational
forms across history, and which will exist in some form
regardless of what happens to *the job* as we currently know
it.

## The decomposition the framework wants to install

The framework's recommendation is that the *AI and labor*
question be decomposed jointly along two axes: *which AI*
(per Chapter 5) and *which labor*. The result is a matrix
that produces actionable analytic distinctions.

On the *which AI* axis:

- **Recommender systems** silently reorganized hiring, lending,
  news, dating, and increasingly judgment itself for roughly
  fifteen years (Chapter 6). The reorganization continues.
- **Generative LLMs** are restructuring the production of
  documents, code, customer-service interactions, and certain
  kinds of analysis on a much more visible timescale (Chapter
  7). The restructuring is most acute in the white-collar
  drafting layer.
- **Vision and biometric models** are mostly silent except where
  they hit a sensor (a face-recognition wrongful-arrest case, a
  targeting system named in court). They are doing substantial
  work in surveillance, inspection, and security industries.
- **Robotic systems with embedded learning** in warehouses,
  agriculture, and logistics are gradient-invisible to the
  professional class because they do not touch their work.
  They are conspicuously visible to the workers whose
  conditions they are reorganizing.

On the *which labor* axis:

- **High-status credentialed knowledge work** — partners at law
  firms, senior physicians, senior engineers, tenured
  academics, senior managers — experiences AI mostly as a tool
  that lets them produce more output per unit of time. The
  job is not threatened. The work is augmented. The income is
  preserved. The status is preserved.
- **Mid-tier credentialed work** — paralegals, junior doctors
  in administrative roles, junior engineers, junior creative
  workers, customer-service operators, content moderation
  back-office workers — is being squeezed first and quietest.
  This layer usually does not produce a strike; it produces
  attrition. People who would have entered these roles five
  years ago are not entering them now. People who are in
  them are being asked to take on more work for the same
  pay. The trajectory is downward without producing a moment
  of public resistance.
- **Working-class service work** — care work, food service,
  retail, cleaning — is largely insulated from generative AI
  but is being progressively reorganized by recommender-system
  scheduling software (variable hours, last-minute shift
  changes, gig-economy intermediation) and by robotic systems
  in the relevant subsectors (warehouse, kitchen, delivery).
  The political register here is different again: visible
  enough to produce strikes (UPS, Amazon warehouse workers,
  the Hollywood IATSE crew) but mostly under-covered in the
  AI-and-labor public conversation.
- **Low-status data work** — the human reinforcement that
  makes the models behave at all, paid at $1.32–$2/hour in
  Kenya at the time of writing, with documented psychological
  harms from exposure to violent imagery during content
  moderation training — is an entire labor category that the
  contemporary AI economy depends on and that the AI-and-labor
  conversation almost never names. The visibility curve is
  inverse to the consequence curve.

These are four different gradients and four different political
responses. Calling all of them *AI and jobs* is, structurally,
the same move as calling everything from a thermostat to ChatGPT
*AI*: a refusal to decompose that is not neutral. It is the
move that lets the high-status discourse absorb the public
attention while the consequential reorganization happens at the
mid-tier and the low-status layers without it.

## Who got which job in the first place

There is one more decomposition the framework needs to install
before this chapter can close. Who got which job in the current
arrangement was never a measurement of natural capacity. It
was a function of race, gender, citizenship, language,
schooling access, and whose children were allowed into which
credential pipeline. The *knowledge worker* category that
contemporary AI discourse most often centers — paralegal,
junior copywriter, customer-service operator, illustrator,
translator, programmer — is a particular layer of a particular
society at a particular moment. The post-WWII professional
middle class is younger than penicillin.

AI does not disrupt the assignment that produced this layer.
**It operates through it**. The mid-tier credentialed workers
being squeezed first by generative AI are
disproportionately the people who entered the professional
middle class most recently — first-generation college
graduates, women who entered male-dominated professions in
the 1990s and 2000s, people of color who entered the
white-collar economy through credentialing pathways
established only in the past few decades. The
high-status credentialed workers being augmented rather than
threatened are disproportionately the people whose families
have been inside the relevant institutions for generations.

This is not a coincidence. It is the consequence of how
inherited capacity stratifies in a society that has been
operating on the unspoken understanding that capacity is
internal and individual rather than environmental — Chapter
1's argument again, returning at a different register. AI is
applying its restructuring force unevenly across a labor
market that was already unevenly stratified. The
restructuring is making the stratification visible at a
register that was previously invisible. Whether the
visibility produces deliberate political response or
absorbs back into the inherited frame is one of the open
questions of the moment.

## What the right question actually is

The reframing the chapter wants to install is straightforward.
Instead of *will AI take my job?*, the questions worth asking
are:

*Which work was load-bearing in the first place?* — meaning,
which of the activities currently bundled together as *jobs*
actually serve a function that someone other than the
employer values, versus which exist primarily for managerial,
political, or status reasons.

*Which actors specifically benefit from the reorganization
happening at the gradient it is happening at?* — meaning,
which of the specific changes underway would be politically
contestable if they were happening on a steeper gradient,
and who is invested in keeping the gradient shallow.

*Whose conditions are getting worse without producing a
moment of public attention?* — meaning, where in the labor
hierarchy is the squeeze happening, and what would it take
to bring those conditions into the same conversation as the
high-status concerns that currently dominate.

*What organizational forms — unions, professional
associations, mutual-aid networks, cooperative ownership
arrangements — actually have the institutional capital to
do the §4 decomposition under bargaining?* — meaning, who
has the capacity to negotiate the specific terms of how AI
gets deployed in their industry, as the SAG-AFTRA and WGA
contracts demonstrated is possible.

These are the questions that produce actionable answers. The
*will AI take my job* question, in its undifferentiated form,
mostly produces hand-wringing and consultant invoices.

## What this part of the book has done

Part II of the book (Chapters 5–8) has done one specific
thing: it has applied the §4 decomposition to *AI* and the
parallel decomposition to *labor*, and shown what happens
when both decompositions are performed together. The result
is a reading of the contemporary moment that is, the framework
believes, more useful for the reader than the standard
doom-and-hype framing.

Part III (Chapters 9–12) turns to what to do. Chapter 9
takes the question of compression and silent versioning —
what happens to the discourse layer through which we used to
disagree, when more and more of the disagreement is mediated
by privately governed models. Chapter 10 takes the political
pathology that lets institutions invoke an unconsulted public
to justify declining to analyze any of this. Chapter 11
takes the tension the framework is trying to hold without
resolving — not against AI as a frontend, but against
monoculture in the frontend layer. Chapter 12 closes with
the specific political problem the framework has not yet
named: the relational physics of bringing this analysis into
rooms not yet ready for it.

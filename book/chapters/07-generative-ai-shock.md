# Chapter 7 — Generative AI as the Visible Edge

ChatGPT was released to the public on November 30, 2022. It
reached one million users in five days. By January 2023 it had
reached 100 million monthly active users — the fastest consumer
adoption curve of any product in recorded technological history.
By the spring of 2023, the financial press had begun referring
to *the AI moment*. By the summer, every Fortune 500 company had
issued a public statement about its AI strategy. By the autumn,
every major government had announced an AI policy initiative.
By 2024, frontier-AI capital expenditure across the major
hyperscalers exceeded the combined R&D budgets of the global
pharmaceutical industry.

None of this happened on a slow gradient. All of it happened
in roughly eighteen months. It is the closest thing to a
genuine paradigm-shift moment, in Kuhn's strict sense, that
the technology sector has produced in this century — and even
that comparison underestimates the speed, because Kuhn's
paradigm shifts in science took decades, not months.

This chapter is about that shock window. The framework's
central observation is that the visibility of generative AI
should not be confused with its consequence — Chapter 6 made
that case at length — but the visibility itself was real, and
what was done during the brief window in which the public
attention was focused matters. A great deal was decided
during that window. Some of what was decided was inevitable.
Some of it was the reflexive use of the shock interval by
actors who had been ready for it. Distinguishing the two is
one of the things this chapter will try to do.

## What actually happened in the shock window

The eighteen months from November 2022 to mid-2024 produced
the following structural changes, each of which would, under
ordinary deliberative conditions, have merited months or
years of public debate:

- Five frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta,
  xAI) consolidated as the dominant institutional locus of
  contemporary AI research. The previous distribution — across
  many research universities, several national labs, and a
  larger number of corporate research divisions — collapsed
  into a much smaller set of actors with much greater
  concentration of capital, talent, and compute.

- Approximately $300 billion in announced capital expenditure
  flowed into AI-specific data-center construction across the
  major hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon, Oracle).
  By 2026 the announced total exceeded $700 billion against
  current AI-related revenue of roughly $40–60 billion — a
  structural subsidy ratio that would not survive any
  conventional capital-discipline review and that has
  nevertheless been treated by the public conversation as a
  vote of confidence in the technology rather than as a bet
  by a handful of companies on their own future market
  position.

- The U.S. federal government issued an Executive Order on
  AI (October 2023), substantially superseded by a subsequent
  Executive Order in early 2025; the European Union finalized
  the AI Act (March 2024); the U.K. AI Safety Institute was
  established (November 2023); China issued the Interim
  Measures for Generative AI Services (July 2023). Each of
  these regulatory moves was substantively important. None of
  them passed through the kind of multi-year deliberative
  process that comparable structural regulation in other
  industries had previously required.

- Major content-licensing deals with publishers (Associated
  Press, News Corp, Vox Media, the Financial Times, others)
  were signed at terms that permanently reshaped the
  relationship between editorial publishing and AI training.
  None of these deals were public-comment exercises.

- A wave of class-action and corporate lawsuits over training-
  data copyright (the Authors Guild case, the New York Times
  case, the Getty case, the Sarah Silverman case, others)
  established the legal landscape that will govern the next
  decade of generative-AI development. Each of these cases
  required millions of dollars to litigate and produced
  precedent on issues that had not been argued at all in
  legislatures.

- The Hollywood writers' (WGA) and actors' (SAG-AFTRA) unions
  conducted ~148-day-each strikes that produced contracts
  decomposing AI into specific operational sub-cases (digital
  replicas, digital alterations, synthetic performers; AI is
  not a writer; literary-material exclusion). These contracts
  are the most carefully decomposed AI policy instruments
  produced anywhere in the world during the shock window.

- Educational institutions issued, then in many cases
  reversed, blanket bans on student use of generative AI tools.
  The most-documented case is the New York City Department of
  Education ban (January 3, 2023) and reversal (May 18, 2023),
  in which Chancellor David C. Banks explicitly named the
  original ban as *knee-jerk fear*. We will return to this
  case in Chapter 10.

- A coalition of frontier-lab leaders and adjacent
  intellectuals called for a *six-month moratorium* on the
  training of AI systems more powerful than GPT-4 (the Future
  of Life Institute open letter, March 2023). The moratorium
  did not happen; the letter did substantial work in framing
  the public discourse around AI as a question of existential
  risk rather than as a question of present-tense harm.

This is not an exhaustive list. It is the partial list of
consequential decisions that were made during the eighteen-month
shock window with a degree of public deliberation that, in any
non-shock period, would have been called inadequate. Some of the
decisions will probably hold up well over the long arc. Others
will probably look, in retrospect, like the kind of decisions
that get made in the disoriented period after a major shock and
that subsequent generations have to spend decades unwinding.

## The shape of a shock window

The framework's argument about all of this is the one introduced
in Chapter 3 and elaborated through the discussion of Klein in
that chapter: the shock window is itself a structural feature of
how change happens, and it is increasingly understood by
sophisticated actors as a *resource* to be planned for and used.

The Friedman doctrine — *only crisis produces real change; when
the crisis occurs, the actions that are taken depend on the ideas
that are lying around* — applies cleanly to the post-November-2022
period. The actors who had been preparing for years to act on
generative AI were ready when the shock arrived. The actors who
had been preparing for years to oppose particular AI deployments
were also ready. The actors who had not been preparing — most
constituencies, most legislatures, most newsrooms, most schools,
most nonprofits — were not.

The actions that were taken during the window were therefore
disproportionately the actions of the prepared. Some of the
prepared were the frontier labs, who had spent a decade building
the regulatory-engagement infrastructure that let them shape
what *AI safety* meant in policy contexts. Some of the prepared
were the unions whose AI-specific contract demands had been
worked out in advance and were ready when the strike windows
opened. Some of the prepared were the doom-AI coalition, whose
decade of essays and conferences and policy-paper writing had
produced a vocabulary of *existential risk* that became the
default framing of the moment. Some of the prepared were the
hype-AI coalition, whose decade of *transformative* and
*civilizational* and *AGI* language was ready to absorb the
public attention in the opposite direction.

The unprepared actors — the constituencies, the educators, the
mid-tier nonprofits, the regional press, the public — were
mostly absent from the window's structural decisions. Not
because they did not care. Because the window opened and
closed faster than the conditions for their participation
could be assembled.

## The framework's own implication in the moment

This book is itself a product of the shock window. The
LeResearch organization that produced the framework was
conceived during a period in which certain conversations
became suddenly possible, certain funding flows opened,
certain institutional positions became available. Honesty
requires that the framework name its own implication in the
dynamic it is critiquing. We are not above the shock-and-
normalize cycle. We are inside it, like everyone else.

What this means concretely is that some of the framework's
own framings — the *frontend* concept, the *decompose-the-
category* posture, the cross-substrate work in education,
hydrology, and AI epistemics — would not have crystallized
in the form they did without the post-November-2022 window.
The framework is, in part, a response to the window's
distinctive opportunity. That is not a confession; it is an
empirical observation.

The implication for how to read the rest of the book is that
the framework does not claim to be neutral. It claims to be
*specific* — about which AI it is talking about when, about
which empirical claim it is borrowing from whom, about
which political position it is taking and on what grounds.
The neutral position does not exist for anyone living
through the window. The honest position is to say what
position you are taking and why.

## What the doom-and-hype framing does

The single most important thing the shock window did, beyond
the specific institutional decisions listed above, was to
install in the public discourse two competing framings that
have, in the years since, dominated almost every public
conversation about AI: *AI doom* and *AI hype*.

*AI doom* holds that the trajectory of frontier-AI capability
development leads, on a relatively short timescale, to systems
whose behavior cannot be reliably aligned with human interests
and that therefore pose an existential or near-existential
risk to humanity. The position has internal variations
(Eliezer Yudkowsky's nearly-certain doom, the Open Philanthropy
moderate-probability framing, Stuart Russell's more measured
*provably beneficial AI* program, Dan Hendrycks's
*catastrophic risk* register, and so on) but converges on the
claim that present-tense regulatory and political effort
should be focused primarily on preventing the development of
systems above some capability threshold.

*AI hype* holds that the trajectory of frontier-AI capability
development leads, on a relatively short timescale, to systems
that will fundamentally restructure the economy, society, and
politics, primarily for the better, and that present-tense
regulatory and political effort should be focused primarily on
ensuring that the restructuring happens fast enough for the
incumbent country (usually the United States, sometimes
substituted with *the West*) to maintain its lead. The
position has internal variations (Sam Altman's gradual-
deployment-with-safety register, Marc Andreessen's
techno-optimist register, Reid Hoffman's *amplified-humanity*
register, and so on) but converges on the claim that the
default policy should be to enable rather than to constrain.

The framework's observation about both framings is that they
share a structural feature: *neither addresses present-tense
harms documented in the recommender-systems literature*. The
doom framing displaces attention forward to hypothetical
future systems. The hype framing displaces attention forward
to hypothetical future benefits. Both displacements have the
same effect on the present: they crowd out the conversation
about specific deployments that are doing specific harms to
specific people right now.

This is the framework's *discourse displacement* observation.
It is not a claim that doom or hype concerns are intrinsically
illegitimate. Some of the doom concerns are substantively
worth taking seriously; some of the hype claims are
substantively right about what specific systems can do. The
claim is structural: the dominance of the doom-and-hype
framings in the public conversation is doing predictable work
to advantage the actors whose business model depends on
attention being focused anywhere except on what is happening
in their own deployments.

## The asymmetric beneficiaries

Both framings, when traced back to their funding and
institutional support, are largely supported by the same
actors. The doom-AI coalition's major institutional vehicles
(MIRI, the Future of Life Institute, the various AI-safety
research organizations, much of the Effective Altruism-aligned
AI-policy infrastructure) have been substantially funded by
donors who are also major investors in the frontier labs.
Anthropic was founded by ex-OpenAI researchers including
several who came out of the AI-safety research community; its
Series A funding included substantial contributions from
EA-aligned donors. The pattern is documented in the public
record and is not particularly contested.[^ch7-1]

The hype-AI coalition's major institutional vehicles
(a16z, the broader VC ecosystem investing in AI applications,
the consultancy class advising large enterprises on AI
adoption) have also been substantially funded by actors who
benefit directly from the framing. This is not a conspiracy
observation. It is a structural one: the doom and hype
framings both serve the interests of capital allocation that
flows through the frontier-lab layer, because both framings
locate the action there. The recommender-systems harms that
do not flow through the frontier-lab layer — the ones whose
business models predate the current frontier and would not
benefit from frontier consolidation — get less institutional
support in either framing.

The framework calls this the *pincer*: doom and hype appear
to be opposed but do the same structural work, displacing
attention from the present-tense harms that neither framing
has incentive to address.

## What the chapter wants the reader to take

The reader does not need to become an expert on the shock
window to read the rest of this book. The reader does need to
hold three observations from this chapter:

First: *generative AI's visibility is not its consequence*.
The most consequential AI deployment of the past fifteen
years was the one Chapter 6 covered. The most visible AI
deployment of the past three years is the one this chapter
covered. They are not the same thing.

Second: *the shock window did real work*. Decisions were
made. Some of them will hold up; some of them will be
unwound by future generations. The actors who were prepared
for the window shaped the decisions. The actors who were
not prepared mostly were not at the table.

Third: *the doom-and-hype framing structurally displaces
the conversation that would matter most for ordinary
readers* — the conversation about what is being done in
their workplaces, schools, hospitals, courts, and welfare
offices, by which specific systems, on whose behalf. The
framework's job, for the rest of the book, is to keep that
conversation in view.

The next chapter takes the question of *labor* directly,
which is where almost every reader of this book will
encounter the personal version of these structural
observations. *AI and labor* has been one of the most-
discussed framings of the moment. Chapter 8 argues that
*AI and labor* is mostly the wrong question, and proposes
the right one.

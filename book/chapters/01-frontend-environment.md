# Chapter 1 — The Frontend Has Always Been Part of the Environment

A teacher writes a problem on the board. Half the class understands it
within seconds. The other half does not. The half that understood is
called *fast*; the half that did not is called *slow*. The teacher does
not believe she is making a moral judgment. She is reporting what she
observes — *who got it* and *who did not*. The classroom moves on.

By the end of the year, the *fast* half will have been treated as
deserving of more attention, more advanced material, more confidence
in their own minds. The *slow* half will have absorbed something
quieter and more durable: that the world's structures of explanation
are not for them, and that this is their own failing rather than the
structure's. By the end of high school, this absorption will have
calcified into self-image. By the end of college (for those who get
there), into career. By mid-career, into the way they speak about
themselves at parties. By retirement, into a life-long story about
who they are.

We have called the result *intelligence*. We have built standardized
tests to measure it, university admissions to select for it, salary
bands to reward it, and casual conversation to reproduce it. We have
treated it as a measurement of the person. It is not. It is, almost
entirely, a measurement of the fit between a particular person and a
particular *frontend*.

This book is about that frontend, and about what is happening to it
right now.

## The argument in one paragraph

The way knowledge is offered — the textbook, the lecture, the
classroom layout, the language used, the pace of presentation, the
level of jargon, the instruments through which a question can be
asked and an answer received — is not a neutral pipe. It is a
*frontend*: a structured filter that determines who gets through and
who does not. Almost every confident statement we make about who is
*smart*, *talented*, *able to learn*, or *suited* to a profession
turns out, on inspection, to be a statement about the fit between a
particular person and a particular frontend. The frontend has always
been part of the environment in which capacity is shaped. We are
only now, in the middle of a generational technological shift, in a
position to see this clearly enough to redesign it.

## What Sapolsky's work actually says

The clearest scientific defense of this claim is in the work of the
neurobiologist Robert Sapolsky, particularly in *Behave: The Biology
of Humans at Our Best and Worst* (2017) and *Determined: A Science
of Life Without Free Will* (2023). Sapolsky's project, across thirty
years of writing and teaching, has been to integrate neuroscience,
endocrinology, evolutionary biology, anthropology, and behavioral
science into a single picture of why human beings do what they do.
The conclusion that picture lands on, again and again, is one most
people find uncomfortable: human capacity is not fixed at birth.
What you can do, what you become competent at, what you find easy or
hard, what you are *like* — all of it is shaped continuously by the
environment you encounter, at every timescale from the milliseconds
of neural firing to the millennia of cultural inheritance.

Sapolsky's word for this is *cascading causation*. A behavior that
looks like character — *she gave up; he persisted; they did not
understand* — is the visible tip of a cascade that runs from
hormones (minutes ago), to nutrition and sleep (hours ago), to
relationships and stress (years ago), to childhood adversity
(decades ago), to gestational environment (before birth), to genes
in the population (millennia ago), to the climate that selected
those genes (millions of years ago). At every level, the
environment is doing something. The person doing the behavior is
not the source of the behavior in the way folk psychology imagines.
The person is where many environments meet.[^1]

This is uncomfortable in part because it sounds like determinism.
Sapolsky is happy to call it that. *Determined* makes the case
explicitly: there is no philosophically defensible spot in the
cascade where you can locate the *self* doing the choosing
independent of everything that produced the self. The book's
critics object on philosophical grounds. The empirical case
Sapolsky assembles, however, is not in serious dispute among the
relevant scientists. Capacity is environmental. We have known this
for a long time. We have not built our institutions as if we knew
it.

The implication for the classroom — and for the courtroom, the
hospital, the loan committee, the hiring panel — is that the
filter is doing more work than we usually give it credit for. The
people who fit through it are not, on average, *more able* in some
intrinsic sense. They are *better matched* to the specific filter
the institution happens to use. Change the filter and you change
the population that fits.

## The historical contingency

The filter we currently use was not handed down on stone tablets.
It was built. The post-industrial classroom — one teacher at the
front, thirty students in age-graded rows, fifty-minute periods,
single canonical text, single language, single pace — is not a
discovery about how learning works. It is a Prussian and
American-industrial decision from the middle of the nineteenth
century, designed for the specific purpose of producing a
literate, punctual workforce capable of factory shift work.[^2]
It worked, in that sense. It still works, in that sense. What it
does not do — and was never designed to do — is provide an
environment in which the full range of human capacity can develop.

It is worth pausing on this. The classroom we are in right now,
the one our children are in, is the same classroom that was built
to produce factory workers in 1850. The factory has changed; the
classroom has not. We have inherited a frontend designed for an
economic and social order that no longer exists, and we have been
running it for so long that we have come to mistake it for
*education* itself.

The same observation applies to the medical clinic (designed
around the assumptions of pre-imaging medicine and pre-statistical
diagnostics), the legal system (designed around the assumption of
literate populations adjudicated by literate professionals at the
pace of postal mail), the corporate office (designed around 1914
Ford 8-hour-day conventions and the 1950s American middle-manager
norm), and the university (designed around medieval guild
structures with a Humboldtian-research overlay added in the
nineteenth century).

Each of these institutions filters who gets through. Each filter
selects for fit between the person and the frontend, not for some
underlying capacity to do the work the institution is nominally
about. The doctor is not the smartest person in the room; she is
the person who fit through medical school's selection filter,
which selected for memorization, for tolerance of authoritarian
hierarchy, for ability to study in particular sustained ways, and
for cultural and economic resources sufficient to survive a long
training pipeline. The lawyer is similar. The teacher is similar.
The engineer is similar.

This is not a complaint about doctors, lawyers, teachers, or
engineers. It is an observation about who else is qualified to do
the work — *would have been* qualified, if the frontend had been
designed differently — and is currently doing other work, or no
work, because the frontend was not designed for them.

## The moral character of the claim

If capacity is environmental, then a society that fails to provide
the environment is not failing to *recognize* talent that exists
inside its members. It is failing to *develop* capacity that would
have existed had the environment been adequate. The difference
matters. The first framing locates the responsibility in the
member: *you should have been better, given what we offered you*.
The second locates it in the society: *we should have offered you
more, given what you could have become*.

The second framing is what the empirical record supports. The
first framing is what most contemporary institutions still
operate on. This gap is not a misunderstanding to be cleared up.
It is a substantive moral claim that has been quietly absorbed
into the operational logic of our schools, hospitals, workplaces,
and courts. Nobody believes, when stated explicitly, that
*structural failure to provide an adequate environment is the
fault of the person who failed to thrive in the inadequate
environment*. Almost everybody acts that way.

This book will return to this asymmetry repeatedly. It is the
moral claim that runs underneath every other argument the book
makes. If the frontend is part of the environment — which it has
always been — then the design of the frontend is a public-health
question, a public-mind question, and only incidentally a
user-experience question. Nobody gets to wash their hands of it
by saying *we are just building software*.

## What the frontend is not

A frontend is not an interface in the narrow technical sense.
The lecture hall is a frontend; so is the textbook, the
multiple-choice test, the office hours appointment system, the
peer-reviewed journal article, the medical-record form, the
courtroom procedure for cross-examining a witness. Anything
through which a person encounters knowledge, expertise, or
institutional decision-making is a frontend. The word is
deliberately broad because the phenomenon is broad.

Nor is a frontend the same as a teacher, a doctor, or a lawyer.
The expert plays a role inside the frontend — sometimes the
central role — but the expert is not the frontend. The
frontend is the structured environment within which the expert
and the learner (or patient, or client) meet. When we say the
frontend has always been part of the environment, we are not
saying that teachers, doctors, and lawyers do not matter. We
are saying that *what they can offer* is constrained by the
frontend through which they offer it, and that the constraint
has, until very recently, been so deeply naturalized that we
forgot it was there.

## Why this is the right place to start

The book begins here because every other argument it will make
depends on this one. If you do not accept that capacity is
environmental, then the rest of the book reads as a series of
unfair complaints about institutions that are doing their best
with the talent they were given. If you do accept it, then the
rest of the book reads as a description of a frontend layer that
is — right now, in the middle of the AI moment — being
reorganized at a pace and in a direction that nobody has been
allowed to deliberately choose.

The chapters that follow will name the inherited frames that
have calcified into infrastructure, the cognitive mechanism by
which slow change becomes invisible to us, the imagined orders
that scale group cooperation past Dunbar's limit, the analytic
black hole the word *AI* has become, the silent reorganization
of work that has been going on for fifteen years inside our
recommender systems, the visible shock that arrived in late 2022
and what was done with the shock window, the labor reorganization
that is happening underneath the discourse, the compression of
truth into a small number of silently versioned models, and the
specific pathology by which institutions invoke an unconsulted
public to justify declining to analyze any of this.

The chapters will also name what to do, with an honest accounting
of how rare and how hard the doing is.

But all of it begins from this one claim, which is the only one
this book will argue for at length without later qualification:

> The frontend has always been part of the environment in which
> capacity is shaped. The design of the frontend is a moral
> question. Nobody gets to wash their hands of it.

If this claim has always been true, then the next question is
why it has stayed invisible for so long. That is the question
of Chapter 2: how inherited frames calcify into infrastructure
that no one remembers having decided.

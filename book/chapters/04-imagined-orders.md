# Chapter 4 — Imagined Orders, From Dunbar to Print to Algorithm

The first three chapters have been about individuals and small
institutions. The frontend a child encounters in a classroom is one
person at a time. The eight-glasses-of-water rule is one person at
a time. Even the normalization gradient that absorbs slow change
into the inherited frame is, in the first instance, a thing that
happens inside a single nervous system. None of this gets us to
nations, religions, markets, professional fields, or the discourse
about AI — units of cooperation involving millions of people who
will never meet, never know each other's names, and never directly
verify what each other claims to be doing.

How human beings came to coordinate at those scales — and what is
happening to the coordination infrastructure right now — is the
subject of this chapter. The argument has two halves. The first is
that scaling cooperation past direct social cognition has always
required a particular technology: shared fictions powerful enough
to substitute for personal knowledge among strangers. The second is
that the specific technology providing those shared fictions
changes, that each historical change reorganizes which kinds of
cooperation become possible and which die out, and that we are in
the middle of one such change right now.

## The Dunbar limit and what crosses it

The British anthropologist Robin Dunbar, working through the 1990s,
proposed on cognitive and ethnographic grounds that the size of
stable social groups in human beings tops out at roughly 150
individuals. Dunbar's number was originally derived from the
relationship between primate brain size and group size; it was
then triangulated against the typical sizes of hunter-gatherer
bands, military companies, sustainable village communities, and
contemporary friend networks. The exact number is debated. The
order of magnitude is not. Direct social cognition — the kind that
lets you know who someone is, who their parents are, what they
owe whom, what they did last week — does not scale past a few
hundred people in any setting that has been carefully studied.[^ch4-1]

This is not just a quaint observation about pre-modern life. It
is the central problem any society has to solve if it wants to do
anything more complex than what 150 people can directly
coordinate. The Roman empire was not 150 people. The Catholic
Church was not 150 people. The Mongol postal system was not 150
people. The contemporary nation-state, the multinational
corporation, the global financial system, the AI investment
cycle — none of them are 150 people. All of them are, in some
form, *solutions* to the problem of cooperation past Dunbar's
limit. None of those solutions are obvious. None of them are
free. All of them depend on a technology that the framework will
call, for the rest of this book, an *imagined order*.

## What an imagined order actually is

The popular vocabulary of *imagined orders* came into general
circulation through Yuval Noah Harari's *Sapiens* (2011 in
Hebrew, 2014 in English). Harari's claim is that what made
*Homo sapiens* uniquely capable of large-scale cooperation was
the species-level capacity to coordinate around shared fictions:
money, gods, nations, corporations, human rights. None of these
exist in physics; all of them function as if they did, because
enough people believe enough of the same things at the same
time. The argument is genuine and useful. The specific
formulation is also breezy where the underlying material is
careful, and we will use the vocabulary while pointing at more
precise sources.[^ch4-2]

The earlier and more precise sources are several. Cornelius
Castoriadis, working in Paris through the second half of the
twentieth century, named the substrate of meaning that any
society depends on the *social imaginary* — the layer of
significations through which a collective decides what counts
as a person, a god, a debt, a kinship relation, a productive
activity. Castoriadis's central distinction was between the
*instituted* and the *instituting* — the difference between a
society reproducing the inherited frame and a society
recognizing that the frame is its own creation and acting to
remake it.[^ch4-3] The American analytic philosopher John Searle,
working from a different tradition, named the same observation
with the formula *X counts as Y in context C* — a piece of
paper *counts as* a twenty-dollar bill in the context of a
society that maintains the institutional fact of currency. The
counting-as relation is what makes institutional reality
reproducible, and it is what makes it changeable, since the
relation can in principle be revoked.[^ch4-4]

What both vocabularies — Castoriadis's continental, Searle's
analytic — point at is that *imagined* in *imagined orders* is
not a softening word. Money is real. Marriage is real. Nationhood
is real. None of them exist in physics, and all of them have
the kind of objectivity that prevents you from paying your rent
in beach pebbles or marrying yourself or claiming citizenship by
declaration. The objectivity rests on collective intentionality.
That objectivity is what makes the orders possible to scale past
Dunbar. That same objectivity is what makes them difficult, but
not impossible, to revise.

## Berger and Luckmann's micro-mechanism

The previous chapter introduced Peter Berger and Thomas Luckmann's
three-step rhythm — *externalization*, *objectivation*,
*internalization* — as the micro-scale mechanism by which inherited
frames calcify into infrastructure. The same rhythm is what
produces and sustains imagined orders at scale. An action is
performed for a specific reason; the action becomes a typification
(*this is what people like us do in this situation*); the
typification becomes an institution (*this is what is done*); the
institution becomes background reality (*this is how the world
works*) for the new members who encounter it. The logic of the
twenty-dollar bill, the marriage license, the academic credential,
the professional certification, the corporate charter, the national
flag, the customs border, the criminal sentence — all of them get
produced through this rhythm and sustained by its continued
operation.[^ch4-5]

Berger and Luckmann assumed the participants in this process were
human, embedded, mortal, and capable of recognizing each other as
co-producers of the institution. The framework will need to ask,
in later chapters, what changes when one of the parties is an
algorithmic system that does not share those conditions of
co-production. For now, the point is that the rhythm has been
running continuously for as long as we have records, and that it
is what produces the imagined orders our societies depend on.

## Anderson and the technology of imagined community

The most concrete demonstration that scaling imagined orders past
Dunbar requires a *technology*, not just a cultural disposition,
came from the political scientist Benedict Anderson, in his 1983
book *Imagined Communities*. Anderson's question was specific: why
did people in late-eighteenth-century Spanish America come to feel
themselves Mexican, Venezuelan, Argentine, when they had
previously felt themselves subjects of the Spanish crown? And why,
once that feeling had taken hold, were people willing to fight
and die for political units that had not existed as objects of
attachment until very recently? The standard answers — economic
interest, language, ethnic identity, anti-colonial mobilization —
were not wrong but did not explain the specifically *imagined*
character of national feeling: the way millions of people who
would never meet came to experience themselves as horizontal
comrades.[^ch4-6]

Anderson's answer was that the nation became possible to feel
because of a specific technological and economic configuration
he called *print capitalism*. Movable type produced a mass market
for printed material. The mass market favored standardization of
vernacular languages — printers could not afford to typeset every
regional dialect, so they converged on a few. The standardized
vernaculars produced communities of readers who could understand
each other across distance. The daily newspaper and the print
novel produced two distinctive forms of *simultaneity*: the
experience of reading the same text on the same morning as
thousands of strangers, and the experience of inhabiting a
fictional time shared with characters one would never meet.
These forms of simultaneity were the cognitive scaffolding on
which the imagined community of the nation became possible to
feel.

The mechanism is the part this book borrows. Anderson is the
cleanest demonstration that *which kinds of imagined community
are possible* is determined by *which synchronization
technologies are available*. Print capitalism produced the
nation. Broadcast television produced the postwar national
audience. Algorithmic feeds are producing — what, exactly?

That question is the bridge from this chapter to the next. The
honest answer is that we do not yet know. The new
synchronization layer is younger than the children currently
in primary school. Its imagined-community products are only
now beginning to take stable enough shape to be named.

## What algorithmic feeds appear to be doing

What we can say, with reasonable confidence, is what the new
layer is *not* producing. It is not producing a single
imagined community of *the public*, of the kind broadcast
television produced through the second half of the twentieth
century. The Walter Cronkite-era assumption that some
substantial fraction of the country was watching the same
news on the same evening, and that what they saw shaped a
shared informational base for democratic deliberation, has
broken down. The algorithmic feed is, by design, personalized;
each viewer sees a feed calibrated to maximize their
engagement with that specific viewer's preferences and
behavior history. The feed is producing a different
informational reality for each viewer, in the same room, at
the same moment.

The political consequence of this is one of the most-discussed
features of the contemporary moment, and we will return to it
in Chapter 9 when we discuss compression and silent versioning.
What matters for the present chapter is the structural point:
the new media regime is producing communities of a different
shape than the previous one, and the imagined orders that
formed under the previous regime — the nation, the public, the
broadcast-era political coalition — are visibly under strain
because their synchronization infrastructure has fragmented.

The *professional middle class* that experiences AI as a
personal threat, and that is the implied audience of much of
this book, is itself an Anderson-style imagined community.
People who do not know each other, who are scattered across
continents, who recognize each other through credentials,
salary bands, magazine subscriptions, podcast tastes, and a
shared sense of being addressed by the same news. The new
media regime is reorganizing that community — not just its
work, but its self-recognition. A reader of this book who
spent the last fifteen years inside a particular informational
diet of newsletters, prestige podcasts, and curated social
feeds is in a *different* imagined community than a reader
who spent it inside Fox News, TikTok, and a regional
megachurch — and both are different communities again from the
broadcast-era *American public* their grandparents inhabited.

This is not a new observation about polarization. It is an
older observation, made by Anderson in 1983, about what the
synchronization layer of a society is doing all the time. We
had not been paying attention to the broadcast layer because
it was infrastructure. We are noticing the algorithmic layer
now mostly because it is failing in spectacular ways and
because the actors who own it are unusually visible. The
quieter observation — the one this book wants to lodge — is
that *which imagined orders are possible to feel*, in a
society, is downstream of *which synchronization technologies
are available*. Change the technology and you change the
orders. We have changed the technology.

## The Castoriadis question, sharpened

Castoriadis's deepest distinction was between the *instituted*
and the *instituting*. Most history runs on the instituted —
the inherited frame is enacted again, slightly modified, and
the participants do not experience themselves as authors of
the frame. The rare and consequential moments are when a
collective recognizes that the frame is its own creation and
acts to remake it. Castoriadis called this capacity *autonomy*,
in a strict sense: the social arrangement in which a collective
acknowledges that its laws are its own creation and can be
revised. Its opposite was *heteronomy*: the arrangement in
which the laws are presented as given by something outside the
collective — by gods, by nature, by the market, by historical
necessity, by *the algorithm*.

Heteronomy is the historical norm. Autonomy is the rare project
of which the Greek polis and the modern democratic revolutions
are exemplars. Castoriadis saw the instituting capacity as the
ground of political freedom, and he was clear that it was
fragile, expensive, and vulnerable to substitution by easier
alternatives.

The framework owes him a position on whether the contemporary
arrangement — in which the conversation about almost everything
is increasingly mediated by privately governed compression
layers, and in which *the AI says* is becoming a sentence
treated the way a previous generation treated *science says* —
is degrading the instituting capacity faster than it is
expanding access to instituted knowledge. The first read is
yes. The proper read needs more care than this chapter can
give it. The framework will return to the question in
chapters 9 and 11.

For now, the structural observation is enough: imagined orders
have always been the technology by which human cooperation
scaled past Dunbar. The technology providing the imagined
orders is changing fast. The orders that formed under the
previous technology are visibly under strain. The orders that
will form under the new technology have not yet stabilized.
The window in which what they become can be deliberately
shaped is open, but only briefly, and only if the people who
might shape it can be persuaded to look up.

## The bridge to Part II

The chapters that follow leave behind the long historical
arc and turn to the specific category that is doing the
most active reorganization of contemporary imagined orders:
the cluster of techniques, deployments, business models, and
political projects that we currently call *AI*. Chapter 5
asks what the word actually refers to, and to whom, and why
the answer matters. Chapter 6 takes the first specific
deployment — recommender systems — and traces the fifteen
years in which they reorganized hiring, lending, news, and
dating without producing the kind of public deliberation
their consequences would otherwise have demanded. Chapter 7
takes the visible edge of the moment — generative AI — and
asks what was actually decided during the shock window of
late 2022 and early 2023, and by whom. Chapter 8 turns to
labor.

What ties Part II together is the recognition that all of
these are happening on the substrate established in Part I.
The frontend is the environment. Inherited frames calcify.
Slow change is invisible. Imagined orders depend on
synchronization technology. We are in a moment in which the
synchronization technology is being rebuilt by a small
number of actors whose business model depends on the
rebuilding, and the rebuilding is happening fast enough to
matter and slow enough to escape sustained public attention.

# AI Fear, Inc.: Who Funds the Doom, Who Benefits, What the Public Actually Worries About

Compiled 2026-04-24. The empirical question: where does AI fear come from, who funds it, who benefits, and is anyone *incentivizing* that fear in ways that serve their interests?

## 1. The Cultural Priors: Doom Is Older Than the Technology

Long before any working AI existed, Western culture had a 200-year-old template for thinking about it: the artificial creation that destroys its creator. Mary Shelley's *Frankenstein* (1818) supplied the moral architecture; Karel Čapek's *R.U.R.* (1920) coined the word "robot" and introduced the uprising; Asimov's *I, Robot* (1940s) tried to legislate the danger away with the Three Laws; *2001: A Space Odyssey* (1968) gave us HAL; *Terminator* (1984) and Skynet supplied the military-AI-as-apocalypse trope; *The Matrix* (1999) and *Ex Machina* (2014) fused it with epistemology and gender. By the time large language models actually shipped in 2022, the public had been pre-loaded with a fully formed narrative for a century.

The intellectual inflection point that turned this folk template into a *policy* category was Nick Bostrom's *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (Oxford University Press, 2014), which became a *New York Times* bestseller and was publicly endorsed by Bill Gates ("highly recommend") and Sam Altman, who in 2015 called it "the best thing he has ever read on AI risks." Bostrom's book is the document that took x-risk from a Bay Area mailing-list concern (more on that below) and made it citable in Davos.

## 2. The Institutional Ecosystem of AI X-Risk

The "AI safety" world is much smaller than its media footprint suggests. A handful of organisations, almost all funded by an overlapping set of donors, generate most of the discourse.

- **MIRI (Machine Intelligence Research Institute)** — Berkeley; founded 2000 as the Singularity Institute by Eliezer Yudkowsky, renamed 2013. Open Philanthropy gave MIRI $2.1M (2019) and supplemented with $7.7M over two years (April 2020); Vitalik Buterin donated several million in ETH in 2021. Yudkowsky's March 2023 *Time* op-ed "Pausing AI Developments Isn't Enough. We Need to Shut It All Down" defines the movement's most extreme pole — he argued for international airstrikes on rogue datacenters.
- **FHI (Future of Humanity Institute)** — Oxford; founded 2005, closed 16 April 2024. Officially "death by bureaucracy" (Bostrom's phrase) after the Faculty of Philosophy froze hiring/fundraising in 2020. The closure was also shadowed by the resurfaced 1996 racist email Bostrom sent to the Extropians list. FHI was the academic mothership — Bostrom, Toby Ord, Will MacAskill, Anders Sandberg all passed through.
- **FLI (Future of Life Institute)** — Max Tegmark; the March 2023 "Pause Giant AI Experiments" open letter (signed by Musk, Wozniak, Yoshua Bengio, Gary Marcus). FLI's funding is dominated by a single $665.8M Shiba Inu coin donation from Vitalik Buterin in May 2021, disclosed in a 2023 EU transparency filing — the donation was *not* publicly known when the Pause letter was issued, and journalists covering FLI in March 2023 had no idea of its scale.
- **CAIS (Center for AI Safety)** — Dan Hendrycks; the 30 May 2023 single-sentence "Statement on AI Risk" ("Mitigating the risk of extinction from AI should be a global priority alongside other societal-scale risks such as pandemics and nuclear war"), signed by Altman, Hassabis, Amodei, Hinton, Bengio, Bill Gates. FTX's lawyers later disclosed the Future Fund had given CAIS $6.5M between May–September 2022, months before FTX's collapse — a clawback target.
- **METR, Apollo Research, Redwood Research** — the "evals" layer. Redwood is candid: $20M of its ~$21M operating budget came from Open Philanthropy, with $1.27M from Tallinn's SFF; its staff and board are interlocked with Open Phil and it works out of the Open-Phil-funded Constellation office.
- **Open Philanthropy** — Cari Tuna and Dustin Moskovitz (Facebook co-founder). Since 2017, OP has given roughly **$2.8B total, of which ~$336M (12%) was AI safety**; in 2023 alone it spent ~$46M on AI safety, the largest single funder in the field. Median AI safety grant: ~$257K; mean ~$1.67M. In 2024 OP committed another ~$50M to technical AI safety.
- **Survival and Flourishing Fund** — Jaan Tallinn (Skype/Kazaa co-founder). Originally seeded 2019 with ~$2M from BERI; has now distributed ~$152M total, **$34.9M in 2025 with 86% to AI safety**, drawn primarily from Tallinn's ~$900M fortune.
- **Anthropic** — Founded 2021 by Dario and Daniela Amodei plus ~9 ex-OpenAI staff. Series A: $124M led by **Jaan Tallinn** with participation from **Dustin Moskovitz**, James McClave, the Center for Emerging Risk Research, and Eric Schmidt — i.e. the AI-safety donor network seeded the company. Holden Karnofsky (Open Philanthropy co-founder, married to Anthropic President Daniela Amodei) joined Anthropic as technical staff in January 2025 to work on the Responsible Scaling Policy.
- **UK AI Safety Institute** — Born from the Frontier AI Taskforce (April 2023, £100M) and announced by Sunak at Bletchley Park in November 2023. The Bletchley Declaration (28 signatory countries including US and China) lifts the CAIS framing nearly verbatim: AI risks "akin to pandemics or nuclear war." This is the single largest moment of *governmental* adoption of the x-risk frame.
- **US AI Safety Institute** — NIST-housed, created under Biden EO 14110 (October 2023).

## 3. The EA → Longtermism → AI Safety Funnel

The talent pipeline runs: Oxford (MacAskill, Ord, Bostrom) → Centre for Effective Altruism / **80,000 Hours** (founded 2011 by MacAskill and Benjamin Todd) → AI safety jobs at MIRI/Anthropic/OpenAI safety teams. MacAskill's *What We Owe the Future* (2022) made the philosophical case for longtermism; 80,000 Hours' career advice ranks "AI safety technical research" as the highest-impact career path.

The **FTX Future Fund** (Feb 2022, run by MacAskill and Nick Beckstead among others) was supposed to industrialise this: it awarded **262 grants totaling $132M in its first ~5 months**, with **~$32M to AI safety** between Feb–Aug 2022, and promised up to $1B/year. It collapsed with FTX in November 2022; the entire team resigned 10 November 2022; many committed grants went unhonored; recipients faced 90-day clawback exposure under bankruptcy law. The FTX collapse is the single most embarrassing event in EA history and severely damaged MacAskill's standing.

The **TESCREAL critique** (Émile P. Torres + Timnit Gebru, *First Monday*, April 2024) argues that Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, and Longtermism form an "interconnected and overlapping" ideological bundle that descends from 20th-century eugenics and functions as a secular eschatology — a Christian-Rapture-shaped narrative used to justify expensive (and currently harmful) projects in the name of saving a hypothetical trillion-person future. The acronym is polemical but the genealogy is documented: Sam Frank's 2015 *Harper's* "Come With Us If You Want to Live" traced the same lineage years before Gebru/Torres named it.

## 4. The Regulatory-Capture / Moat Thesis

This is the load-bearing empirical claim, and it has more documentary support than its critics admit.

**Sam Altman's 16 May 2023 Senate Judiciary testimony** (subcommittee on Privacy, Tech and the Law) is the central exhibit: he proposed *licensing and capability-threshold testing* for models above a certain size, plus a new federal agency. Senator Dick Durbin remarked on the record that he "could not recall a time when representatives for private sector entities had ever pleaded for regulation." The compute-threshold approach, by definition, only binds incumbents who already have the compute — and creates a licensure bottleneck that small competitors would have to clear.

**Marc Andreessen's "Why AI Will Save the World"** (a16z, June 2023) gave the counter-thesis its sharpest formulation: a "Baptists and Bootleggers" frame in which "some of the Baptists are actually Bootleggers" and "there is a profession of 'AI safety expert,' 'AI ethicist,' 'AI risk researcher' who are paid to be doomers." Andreessen called AI risk "a cult."

**Yann LeCun (Meta Chief AI Scientist)** has been the most prominent insider dissenter, publicly calling extinction concerns "complete B.S." (TechCrunch, October 2024) and tweeting in May 2024 that "doomers" want AI "monopolized by a small number of companies under tight regulatory control." LeCun signed a November 2023 open letter from 70+ researchers explicitly accusing OpenAI and DeepMind of seeking "regulatory capture of the AI industry."

**California SB 1047** is the cleanest test case. Sponsored by Senator Scott Wiener; supported by the **Center for AI Safety, Anthropic (after amendments), Elon Musk, the LA Times editorial board**; opposed by **Meta, OpenAI, Google, a16z, House Speaker Nancy Pelosi**, and a coalition of academic AI researchers including Fei-Fei Li. **Newsom vetoed 29 September 2024**, citing the bill's narrow focus on compute-defined "frontier" models as crude — exactly the open-source critique. *Jacobin* framed the veto as "Big Tech beats democracy"; the open-source side framed it as the defeat of an incumbent moat.

**EU AI Act foundation-model lobbying** is the European parallel. France, Germany and Italy — at the explicit direction of **Mistral AI**, whose lobbying in Brussels was led by former French digital state secretary **Cédric O** (himself a Mistral co-founder with direct access to Macron) — pushed successfully for foundation-model exemptions, especially for open-source. The Corporate Europe Observatory documented the lobbying campaign in detail (November/December 2023). The final Act gave broad open-source exemptions, except for models deemed "systemic risk."

**Voluntary frameworks as preemption**: Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy and OpenAI's Preparedness Framework are voluntary; multiple critics (LeCun, Andreessen, Mistral, AI Now Institute) argue these voluntary commitments are designed to head off binding rules while signalling responsibility.

## 5. Who Benefits from the X-Risk Frame — Empirically

This is where motivation must be inferred but the structural beneficiaries are documented:

1. **Frontier labs.** "Only we can build it safely" justifies capital concentration, government access, and licensure barriers. Anthropic's $183B valuation (Fortune, 2025) is explicitly built on the safety brand.
2. **Government safety institutes.** UK AISI (£100M founding budget) and US AISI exist because the x-risk frame was adopted at Bletchley. Their continued budgets depend on it.
3. **OP/SFF-funded researchers.** A professional class whose careers, conferences, and identity are downstream of accepting the funder's worldview. Redwood's near-100% Open Phil dependency is the limit case.
4. **Media.** "AI will kill us all" is a stronger story than "AI is a stochastic statistical pattern matcher with bias problems." The 2023 CAIS one-sentence statement was front-page in NYT, WaPo, BBC, CBC simultaneously — almost no other AI story has had that reach.
5. **Specific incumbent outcomes.** SB 1047 *almost* passed; the EU AI Act *did* pass with foundation-model rules. Both outcomes were vigorously contested by open-source advocates as moats.

## 6. The Counter-Position: Gebru, Bender, DAIR, AI Now

The "Stochastic Parrots" paper (Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, "Shmargaret Shmitchell" [Margaret Mitchell], FAccT 2021) reframed LLMs as systems that "statistically mimic text without understanding," with documented harms from environmental cost, opaque bias, and the labor of curation. Google asked Gebru to retract or remove Google authors' names; she refused; she was fired in December 2020, Mitchell two months later. This is the founding event of the present-harms camp.

Gebru subsequently founded the **Distributed AI Research Institute (DAIR)** (2021) on a paradigm of community-rooted, non-corporate AI research. The TESCREAL paper (with Torres, 2024) is the explicit theoretical attack on the x-risk frame as ideology.

**AI Now Institute** (Whittaker and Crawford, NYU, founded 2017) provides the sociotechnical critique: bias and inclusion, rights and liberties, labor and automation, surveillance amplification. Crawford's *Atlas of AI* (2021) and Whittaker's Signal Foundation work both treat AI primarily as an extension of corporate surveillance power.

**Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson**, *Power and Progress* (2023): the labor-economics version of the critique. Argues current AI is "so-so technology" optimised to automate workers rather than augment them, with predictable consequences for the wage share. Acemoglu won the 2024 Nobel partly for this lineage.

**Karen Hao, *Empire of AI: Dreams and Nightmares in Sam Altman's OpenAI*** (Penguin, May 2025) — built on 260 interviews, won the 2025 NBCC Award and the 2026 Helen Bernstein Book Award; her Kenya/Sama reporting documented the psychological trauma of OpenAI's content moderators paid roughly $2/hr. **Brian Merchant, *Blood in the Machine*** (2023) is the historical companion piece — Luddites as labor activists, not technophobes.

The shared thesis: x-risk discourse is a *distraction* mechanism. By making the salient question "will future AGI respect human values?" rather than "is this Kenyan moderator being psychologically destroyed for $2/hr right now?", it deflects accountability from harms that are already measurable and actionable.

## 7. What Publics Actually Fear (vs. What Elites Talk About)

This is the most important empirical mismatch in the whole story.

**Pew Research** (Aug 2024 survey, published April 2025): **51% of US adults are more concerned than excited about AI** vs. only 11% more excited (up from 37% concerned in 2021). But the *content* of public concern is creative and relational: 53% expect AI to worsen creative thinking; 50% expect AI to worsen meaningful relationships (vs. only 5% improve). 57% rate AI's societal risks as "high." Crucially, only **15% of AI experts** are more concerned than excited — a 36-point gap with the public. Extinction does not appear in the top concerns of either group.

**Reuters/Ipsos** (4,446 US adults, Aug 2025): **71% worry AI will permanently displace workers**; 77% worry about AI-driven political chaos / deepfakes; 66% worry about AI replacing human relationships; 61% concerned about AI energy consumption. Extinction risk is, again, not the headline concern.

The public's fear is concrete: jobs, deepfakes, surveillance, energy, atomisation. The elite policy discourse is abstract: alignment, capabilities thresholds, x-risk. The two are *not the same fear*. The Open Philanthropy/Tallinn-funded ecosystem has successfully shifted regulatory conversation toward the second category, while Pew/Reuters data show the public lives in the first.

## 8. Money Flow Summary (Best-Documented Numbers)

- **Open Philanthropy**: ~$336M cumulative AI safety since 2017; ~$46M in 2023 alone; ~$50M committed in 2024
- **Survival & Flourishing Fund (Tallinn)**: ~$152M cumulative since 2019; ~$34.9M in 2025 (~86% AI safety)
- **FTX Future Fund**: ~$132M in ~5 months in 2022, ~$32M of it AI safety, then collapsed
- **FLI**: holds the Buterin $665.8M SHIB donation (2021)
- **MIRI**: ~$10M reserves at 2025 year-end, primarily from Open Phil + Buterin
- **Anthropic Series A**: $124M led by Tallinn + Moskovitz + Schmidt (2021)
- **UK AISI**: £100M founding budget (2023)

A reasonable conservative aggregate of money explicitly mobilised under "AI x-risk / safety" framing in the past decade is **well over $1 billion** — and that's before counting the Anthropic valuation which is itself a safety-branded outcome.

The ratio that matters: **the annual flow into x-risk-framed work (~$100M+/year from OP+SFF combined) dwarfs the funding available for the present-harms research community** (DAIR, AI Now, academic FAccT-aligned labs operate on single-digit millions). This funding asymmetry shapes which questions get asked.

## 9. The Genealogy: A Small, Identifiable Lineage

Yudkowsky discovered Max More's Extropians mailing list as a teenager in the late 1990s; the same list contained Hans Moravec, Marvin Minsky, Ray Kurzweil, Nick Bostrom, Julian Assange, and several Bitcoin-adjacent figures (Nick Szabo, Wei Dai, Hal Finney). Yudkowsky founded the Singularity Institute (now MIRI); **Peter Thiel began funding it in 2005**, and partnered with Kurzweil on the Singularity Summit. Tallinn co-founded MetaMed with Thiel money and rationalist staff. Bostrom built FHI; FHI alumni built FLI, CAIS, Anthropic safety teams, OpenAI's safety teams, 80,000 Hours, and the EA infrastructure.

The point is *not* that this is a conspiracy; it is that AI x-risk is **not a broad scientific consensus that emerged independently from many directions**. It is the institutional output of a small, ideologically coherent subculture (cypherpunks → extropians → singularitarians → rationalists → EA → AI safety) that successfully captured the policy frame in 2023.

## Final Empirical Answer: Is Anyone Incentivising AI Fear?

**Yes — and the documentation is not subtle.**

1. A small donor network (Open Philanthropy/Moskovitz, Tallinn/SFF, Buterin/FLI, historically FTX/SBF) has channeled **over $1B** into organisations whose core product is x-risk framing, defining the field's professional class, conferences, and career incentives.
2. The same donor network seeded the leading "safety-first" frontier lab (Anthropic Series A: Tallinn lead, Moskovitz participating; Open Philanthropy co-founder Karnofsky now on staff and married to its President), creating a structural alignment between commercial and ideological interests.
3. Frontier-lab CEOs (Altman in Senate testimony; the CAIS one-sentence statement signed by Altman, Hassabis, Amodei) actively requested licensure and compute-threshold regulation that, by construction, raises barriers to smaller competitors.
4. The dissenting position is held mostly by people *outside* this donor network: LeCun (Meta, open-source incumbent with a different commercial interest), Mistral and the French government, Andreessen/a16z, Pelosi and California academics (SB 1047), and the Gebru/Bender/DAIR/AI Now camp (different value system, much smaller funding).
5. The public, per Pew and Reuters/Ipsos, fears jobs/deepfakes/surveillance/atomisation — *not* extinction. The gap between elite policy discourse and public concern is the signature of a successful framing operation.

The honest version is not "evil cabal manufactures fear." It is structural: a coherent ideological subculture, funded by a handful of tech-fortune donors, has built the dominant institutional infrastructure for thinking about AI policy, and that infrastructure happens to produce regulatory frames that benefit the frontier labs whose founders share the subculture's worldview. Whether one calls this **regulatory capture, ideological capture, or "Doom, Inc."** (per *The Logic*'s 2024 investigation), the funding flows, organizational ties, and policy outcomes are documented in primary sources — Form 990s, EU transparency filings, Senate testimony, FTX bankruptcy filings, and the donors' own published grant databases.

The question "is anyone incentivising AI fear?" therefore has a one-word answer: **yes**, with names, dates, and dollar amounts.

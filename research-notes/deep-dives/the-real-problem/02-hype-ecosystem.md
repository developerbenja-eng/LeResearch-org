# The AI Hype / Inevitabilism Ecosystem

Compiled 2026-04-24. Mirror investigation to the doom-ecosystem brief: the "AI inevitabilism" frame — *"AGI is coming, the only question is who builds it first"* — is not a neutral observation about technology. It is a rhetorical infrastructure that unlocks capital, policy concessions, attention, and labor on an extraordinary scale.

## 1. The AGI/inevitabilism canon

The hype ideology has a defined literary canon, mostly written by people whose net worth depends directly on its believability.

**Sam Altman, "Moore's Law for Everything"** (March 2021). Opens the canon by declaring "this technological revolution is unstoppable" and that "the magnitude of socioeconomic change is coming sooner than most people believe." Frame: progress is exogenous and inevitable; the only question is wealth distribution.

**Sam Altman, "The Intelligence Age"** (Sept 23, 2024). "It is possible that we will have superintelligence in a few thousand days." Promises "massive prosperity," "fixing the climate," "establishing a space colony," and "the discovery of all of physics" as eventual commonplaces. Critically pre-commits the reader to infrastructure spend: "If we don't build enough infrastructure, AI will be a very limited resource that wars get fought over."

**Sam Altman, "Reflections"** (Jan 2025): "We are now confident we know how to build AGI as we have traditionally understood it." Pivots target to "superintelligence." Days later: Stargate's $500B announcement.

**Dario Amodei, "Machines of Loving Grace"** (Oct 11, 2024). 14,000-word essay predicting "powerful AI" by 2026 followed by a "compressed 21st century" — 50–100 years of biological progress in 5–10 years, elimination of most cancer, cures for genetic disease.

**Demis Hassabis** has framed DeepMind's mission since 2010 as "step one, solve intelligence; step two, use it to solve everything else" — quoted on a Nobel-laureate's pedestal as of 2024.

**Marc Andreessen, "Why AI Will Save the World"** (June 2023) and **"The Techno-Optimist Manifesto"** (Oct 16, 2023). The latter contains the most aggressive single line in the canon: "Any deceleration of AI will cost lives. Deaths that were preventable by the AI that was prevented from existing is a form of murder."

**Leopold Aschenbrenner, "Situational Awareness: The Decade Ahead"** (June 2024). 165-page essay arguing AGI by 2027 via straight-line extrapolation of compute (~0.5 OOMs/year), algorithmic efficiency, and "unhobbling," followed by an intelligence explosion. Aschenbrenner left OpenAI's superalignment team and immediately raised an AGI-themed hedge fund — the essay is also a fund prospectus.

The unifying move across all these texts is to treat the *trend line* as ontology. None of them seriously engage with the possibility that scaling stalls, that returns on capex flatten, or that "intelligence" doesn't decompose the way they assume.

## 2. Inevitabilism as rhetorical move

Critics have caught up. **Karen Hao's *Empire of AI*** (May 2025, won the 2025 NBCC Award for nonfiction) is the central counter-text. Hao's thesis: "AI companies have created a veneer of inevitability," but "technology doesn't just happen because it comes along — it comes because of choices that people make." She borrows Acemoglu/Johnson's argument that the labor-automating direction of AI is a *choice*, not a natural law.

**Tristan Harris** (Center for Humane Technology) frames his work around breaking people "out of the trance of fatalism and inevitability," explicitly drawing the analogy to social media: "social media's harms weren't inevitable — they were the result of specific design choices."

**Daniel Schmachtenberger** identifies "race dynamics" / Moloch as the *generator function* — the inevitabilism is real *because* multiple actors believe it, not because the technology dictates it. Coordination failure dressed up as physics.

The "if we don't build it, China will" frame is the operational form of this. Notable that **Helen Toner** (former OpenAI board, now CSET) has openly disputed it: she argues "the US is ahead by something like one to two-and-a-half years," and "the Chinese Communist Party doesn't seem especially 'AGI-pilled.'" The race exists in San Francisco's head more than in Beijing's.

**Eric Schmidt's Special Competitive Studies Project** (founded Oct 2021, modeled on Kissinger's Rockefeller Special Studies Project) is the institutional engine of the China-race framing in DC, deliberately echoing Cold War rhetoric. Schmidt simultaneously invests in AI companies and warns Congress about losing to China — a pattern *Tech Transparency Project* has flagged as "cozies up to China's AI industry while warning U.S. of its dangers."

The market itself rewards the rhetoric: FactSet found S&P 500 companies citing "AI" on Q3 2025 earnings calls outperformed those that didn't (13.9% vs. 5.7% YTD by mid-Dec 2025). 306 S&P 500 calls cited AI in Q3 — a 10-year record. The pop is observable, repeatable, and trades on the word itself.

## 4. Promised vs delivered

This is where the inevitabilism story most clearly cracks.

- **McKinsey (June 2023)** projected gen-AI could generate "$2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion" in annual value across 63 use cases. The same report projected labor productivity growth of *0.1%–0.6% annually through 2040* — buried beneath the trillions headline.
- **Daron Acemoglu (NBER WP 32487, May 2024 / *Economic Policy* Jan 2025)** independently estimated "no more than a 0.66% increase in TFP over 10 years," and noted that even this likely overstates because early evidence comes from easy-to-learn tasks. His revised estimate: **<0.53%** TFP gain over 10 years. That is roughly two orders of magnitude smaller than McKinsey's framing implies.
- **MIT NANDA "GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025"**: 95% of enterprise gen-AI pilots delivered "no measurable impact on P&L." Based on 150 interviews, 350 employee surveys, 300 deployment analyses. Despite **$30–40B in enterprise investment**.
- **METR, "Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity"** (July 10, 2025; arXiv 2507.09089): randomized controlled trial, 16 experienced devs, 246 tasks. AI tools made them **19% slower**. Devs *expected* a 24% speedup and *self-reported* a 20% speedup post-hoc — meaning subjective hype-belief survived contradicted measurement.
- **GitHub's own "55% faster" trial** (arXiv 2302.06590, Feb 2023) had a 95% confidence interval of 21%–89% on a single task (implementing an HTTP server in JS) by junior-skewed devs without internet restrictions on the control. Not a generalizable productivity claim; widely cited as if it were.
- **Failed/oversold deployments:** IBM Watson Health (~$4B, sold for parts in 2022 after MD Anderson cancelled the partnership having spent $62M); GM Cruise (>$10B operating losses on <$500M revenue, shut Dec 2024); Babylon Health (delisted, bankrupt 2023); AI radiology firms persistently overpromising replacement of radiologists since 2016.

The pattern is consistent: macro-level claims one to three orders of magnitude larger than measured productivity, and pilot-level failure rates ~95%.

## 5. The "AI is just statistics" deflation

The technical critique runs in parallel.

- **Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, Shmitchell, "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots"** (FAccT 2021). LLMs are "stitching together sequences of linguistic forms... without any reference to meaning." The form/meaning distinction is the cleanest single-paragraph rebuttal of "GPT understands."
- **Subbarao Kambhampati** (ASU, "Can LLMs Really Reason and Plan?" CACM, and arXiv 2402.01817): chain-of-thought is "highly susceptible to the Clever Hans effect — the human in the loop is steering the LLM, even unintentionally." Argues LLMs are "approximate retrieval" engines and reasoning attribution is anthropomorphism.
- **Yann LeCun** (Meta Chief AI Scientist): "LLMs are an off-ramp on the path to AGI." Identifies four missing ingredients: common-sense physics, persistent memory, reasoning, planning.
- **François Chollet, "On the Measure of Intelligence"** (2019) and ARC-AGI (now ARC-AGI-3, 2026): industry "rewards memorization, not novel skill acquisition." Jensen Huang claiming "AGI is achieved" is exactly the failure mode Chollet warned about.
- **Apple, "The Illusion of Thinking"** (machinelearning.apple.com, June 2025; arXiv 2506.06941): on controllable puzzle environments, frontier reasoning models exhibit "complete collapse" at high complexity — and counter-intuitively, *reasoning effort declines* as complexity rises past a threshold, despite available token budget.

The collective force of these critiques is that the AGI timeline rests on extrapolating benchmarks that don't measure what they claim, while the architectures themselves have known structural limitations the canon ignores.

## 6. Inevitabilism as sales tool

The inevitabilism frame has migrated from essays into the GTM motion of every major AI vendor.

- **Pre-commit narrative**: Microsoft, Google, Salesforce, Slack, Adobe — all 2024–25 product announcements lean on "AI is happening with or without you." Copilot pricing and bundling are designed so opting out feels like falling behind.
- **FOMO economy**: enterprise sales decks routinely cite the McKinsey $4.4T figure (rarely the 0.1–0.6% productivity figure).
- **Reskilling industry**: bootcamps, "AI literacy" courses, LinkedIn Learning, executive education programs — all sold against the threat that employees will become "AI-illiterate." This monetizes inevitabilism as personal anxiety.

## 7. Policy capture from the hype side

- **Schumer AI Insight Forums** (Sept 2023 – Dec 2023, 9 sessions): closed-door, 108 attendees of which 44 from industry, 14 from academia. Sen. Warren: "These tech billionaires want to lobby Congress behind closed doors with no questions asked. That's just plain wrong." The first forum's roster: Pichai, Musk, Huang, Zuckerberg, Schmidt, Altman, Nadella, Gates.
- **Stargate Project** (announced Jan 21, 2025 from the White House podium with Altman, Ellison, Son, Trump): $500B over four years, $100B immediately. Operational responsibility OpenAI, financial responsibility SoftBank. Announced in Trump's first week — coordinated regulatory/political theater.
- **Trump AI Action Plan, "Winning the Race"** (July 23, 2025): 90 federal policy positions, three pillars (innovation, infrastructure, international leadership). The plan instructs the FTC to "stand down efforts that may impede the AI industry" and review existing FTC investigations to ensure they don't "advance theories of liability that unduly burden AI innovation." NIST AI Risk Management Framework directed to remove references to misinformation, DEI, and climate. This is **regulatory capture via inevitabilism**: the urgency of "winning" justifies removing brakes.
- **US-UAE AI Acceleration Partnership** (May 2025, Trump Gulf trip): 5GW Abu Dhabi data-center campus (G42 + OpenAI + Oracle + NVIDIA + Cisco + SoftBank), 500K NVIDIA chips/year 2025–27, $200B in announced UAE deals, $600B Saudi commitment. The race frame *unlocks* chip exports to authoritarian states — the very thing export controls existed to prevent.
- **EU AI Act erosion**: The Commission's Nov 2025 "Digital Omnibus" delays high-risk system oversight; GPAI provider enforcement powers don't activate until Aug 2026, with a one-year adjustment window. Corporate Europe Observatory: 69% of Commission AI meetings in 2025 were with business groups, 16% with NGOs.

## 8. The "AGI race" framing — empirical critique

The race frame is empirically weak.

- **Stanford HAI 2026 AI Index**: US/China frontier-model performance gap shrunk to **2.7 percentage points** — small enough that "race" no longer describes the dynamic.
- **Recorded Future 2025**: Chinese gen-AI models trail US by 3–6 months, not 1–2 years.
- **DeepSeek R1** (Jan 2025): trained for ~$6M (vs. estimated $100M+ for GPT-4-class models). The efficiency frontier is shifting against the "more compute = more moat" thesis the inevitabilism canon depends on.
- **Toner** explicitly: the "race" framing carries "problematic background assumptions"; the CCP is not "AGI-pilled."

Interesting tell: even **Sam Bowman** (Anthropic) and other researchers inside the major labs have publicly acknowledged race-frame distortion in interviews and blog posts — but the labs' fundraising decks continue to lean on it.

## 9. Who exits when?

Insider sales are the cleanest signal of internal vs external belief.

- **Jensen Huang (NVIDIA)**: 10b5-1 plans authorized $865M sale (May 2025 plan, on top of June–Sept 2024 sales >$700M). Cumulative dispositions >$2.9B by Oct 2025 (Bloomberg). Still holds ~860M shares (~3.5% of outstanding, ~$150B+ value). Pattern: predictable weekly sales of 25–75K shares while publicly maintaining maximalist demand narratives.
- **OpenAI**: $6.6B employee tender at $500B valuation (Oct 2, 2025) — second tender in <12 months (the first was $1.5B with SoftBank in Nov 2024). Employees are systematically taking liquidity at private-market peaks.
- **NVIDIA broader insider selling**: ~$3.3B in cumulative executive sales documented (Techi compilation, 2024–25).

The hype-makers are converting paper to cash on a steady schedule. Buying with the same urgency: sovereign wealth (MGX, PIF) and SoftBank — entities whose own balance-sheet logic depends on the narrative continuing.

## 10. Is hype documentably incentivized?

**Yes — with multiple converging lines of evidence.**

1. **Mention-pop documented in market data.** S&P 500 companies citing "AI" outperformed non-citers by 8.2 percentage points YTD by mid-Dec 2025 (FactSet). This is a measurable market-side reward for the rhetoric — independent of fundamentals. Saying "AI" on a call literally moves the stock.
2. **Stargate timing.** A $500B private-sector commitment announced from the White House podium in Trump's first week (Jan 21, 2025), with the Trump AI Action Plan following six months later. The "race against China" framing in the canon (Aschenbrenner June 2024 → Schmidt's SCSP) directly produced the policy concession (FTC stand-down, NIST framework de-fanging) within 18 months.
3. **UAE/Saudi chip access.** The "race" rhetoric overrode the export-control logic that had explicitly prevented these very chip flows in 2023. Trump's May 2025 trip yielded 500K NVIDIA chips/year to the UAE plus $200B in deals.
4. **Macro vs micro mismatch.** McKinsey's $4.4T headline (used in every enterprise sales deck) sits on a 0.1–0.6% productivity estimate that is in the same magnitude as Acemoglu's 0.53% TFP-over-a-decade. The trillions are achieved by aggregating tiny per-task gains across the entire global economy under near-perfect adoption assumptions — a methodological choice that produces the headline executives want.
5. **MIT NANDA 95% pilot failure rate** despite $30–40B enterprise spend, alongside METR's RCT showing AI *slows* expert developers by 19% while they *believe* it speeds them up by 20%. The hype survives contradicted measurement, which is the signature of an ideology, not a finding.
6. **Insider selling cadence.** Huang's $2.9B+ in disciplined 10b5-1 sales and OpenAI's two tender offers in 12 months show insiders converting belief-into-cash on a pre-arranged schedule while publicly amplifying the demand narrative.
7. **Consultancy capture.** Accenture's $4.1B FY2025 gen-AI revenue and BCG's $2.7B AI line item exist because the McKinsey-style trillion-dollar framing creates board-level demand for "AI strategy." The same firms that generated the headline numbers monetize them as billable hours.

The honest summary: the AI hype ecosystem is structurally analogous to the AI doom ecosystem. Both rely on framings of inevitability (utopian or apocalyptic), both extract capital and policy concessions from those framings, both have insider participants who personally benefit, and both contain real technical claims worth taking seriously *underneath* the rhetoric. The hype side is currently bigger by orders of magnitude in dollar terms — $600B in 2026 hyperscaler capex, $500B Stargate, ~$84B in 2025 AI mega-rounds — and has captured federal policy in the US, UAE, and Saudi Arabia in coordinated fashion, while the EU's enforcement regime is being actively rolled back.

The question is not whether hype is incentivized. The question is whether any of the actors in the table at section 3 could afford to stop deploying it.
